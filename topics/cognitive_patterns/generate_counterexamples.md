# Generate Counterexamples

## Purpose

Use this pattern to prevent attractive explanations from becoming unfalsifiable stories.

## Use when

- A theory explains many cases too easily.
- An analogy feels persuasive.
- A policy recommendation is based on one success story.
- A concept is being generalized across domains.
- Confidence is rising faster than evidence quality.

## Counterexample types

1. Same cause, different outcome
2. Different cause, same outcome
3. Boundary case
4. Reversal case
5. Historical failure
6. Adversarial case
7. Missing-mechanism case

## Procedure

1. State the claim in falsifiable form.
2. Identify its necessary and sufficient conditions.
3. Search for cases with the same conditions but different outcomes.
4. Search for the same outcome produced by different mechanisms.
5. Construct extreme and boundary cases.
6. Ask what evidence would reverse the conclusion.
7. Refine, narrow, or reject the claim.
8. Record the surviving scope conditions.

## Example

Claim:

> Dense supplier networks create industrial dominance.

Counterexamples to seek:

- dense clusters that stagnated;
- dominant regions with low geographic density;
- clusters that had density but lacked anchor demand;
- cases where density caused congestion or lock-in.

Refined claim:

> Dense supplier networks accelerate capability accumulation when combined with demand, mobility, standards, and rapid feedback.

## Failure modes

- Generating trivial exceptions.
- Treating one anomaly as full disproof.
- Selecting only weak counterexamples.
- Ignoring measurement error.
- Failing to update the original claim.
- Confusing moral objection with empirical counterexample.

## Evaluation

A useful counterexample exercise should:

- change or narrow the original claim;
- identify scope conditions;
- include at least one serious empirical or constructed challenge;
- distinguish exceptions from mechanism failure;
- reduce unjustified confidence.
