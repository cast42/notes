Title: Yellowstone wolves may not have reshaped the national park after all
Author: ScienceDaily

Summarize output:
A new reanalysis argues that one of the most famous Yellowstone stories, that wolves triggered a dramatic park-wide trophic cascade and huge willow recovery, was overstated by flawed methods. The central criticism is that the 2025 study's headline claim, including a reported 1,500% increase in willow crown volume, relied on circular reasoning: plant height was used both to calculate willow volume and then to predict it. In the authors' view, that makes the relationship look strong by construction rather than proving a real biological effect.
*"The relationship is circular"*
The reanalysis also points to several other methodological problems. The original model was applied to heavily browsed willows with odd growth forms it was not designed for, many plots compared between 2001 and 2020 were not actually the same locations, and global comparisons assumed equilibrium conditions that do not fit Yellowstone's still-recovering ecosystem. The authors also say the analysis downplayed other drivers such as hydrology, local site conditions, browsing pressure, and possibly human hunting, which makes a simple wolves-to-willows story harder to sustain.
The broader conclusion is not that wolves do nothing, but that their effects appear smaller, more context-dependent, and more spatially variable than the famous narrative suggests. The new paper also helps explain why earlier researchers working from the same dataset reached very different answers: Ripple et al. saw one of the world's strongest trophic cascades, while Hobbs et al. had already reported only weak effects. This reanalysis sides much more with the latter view and argues that strong ecological claims require stronger statistical evidence than the Yellowstone wolf story has so far provided.
*"Predator effects in Yellowstone are real but context-dependent"*
