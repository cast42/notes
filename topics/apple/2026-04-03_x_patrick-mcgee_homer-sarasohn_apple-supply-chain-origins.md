## TL;DR
Patrick McGee says a key to understanding Apple’s supply-chain strategy runs back not just to Steve Jobs or Tim Cook, but to **Homer Sarasohn**, an American engineer sent to occupied Japan in 1946.

The thread frames Sarasohn’s ideas about process and industrial discipline as an underappreciated ancestor of the supply-chain logic that later helped shape Apple’s famous turnaround.

## Key takeaways
- McGee highlights Homer Sarasohn as a missing historical link in Apple’s manufacturing story.
- The argument connects postwar industrial lessons in Japan to later process thinking inside Apple.
- He positions this as a double-feature story in the *Financial Times*, not fully covered in *Apple in China*.
- The subtext is that Apple’s supply-chain advantage is both foundational and badly misunderstood.

## Summary
In the post, Patrick McGee recounts hearing from an ex-Apple engineer that there should be bronze statues of Homer Sarasohn in Apple Park because so much of Apple’s supply-chain strategy ultimately traces back to what Sarasohn taught in occupied Japan. McGee says he was initially baffled by the connection, but years later pursued it deeply enough to write a two-part *Financial Times* feature.

The thread links several ideas together: Sarasohn’s work in postwar Japan, Steve Jobs’s later discovery of the value of “process,” and the role those ideas played in the decade of Apple’s celebrated corporate turnaround. McGee notes that he originally wanted this material to open *Apple in China*, but structural constraints and book-leave tradeoffs kept it out. He later kept digging through obscure articles and archival materials, including documents at the Library of Congress, and decided Apple’s 50th anniversary was the right moment to publish the story.

The broader takeaway is that Apple’s supply-chain mastery did not emerge from nowhere, nor solely from late-stage operational optimization. It came out of a deeper history of industrial process thinking, quality discipline, and organizational learning—ideas that moved from wartime/postwar engineering contexts into modern tech manufacturing.
