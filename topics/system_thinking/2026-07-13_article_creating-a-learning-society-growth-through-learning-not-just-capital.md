# Creating a Learning Society: growth through learning, not just capital

*Joseph E. Stiglitz and Bruce C. Greenwald*

## TL;DR

- The book argues that the real engine of long-term growth is not just capital accumulation but a society’s ability to learn, innovate, and spread knowledge across firms and sectors.
- Its key conceptual move is to prioritize dynamic efficiency over static efficiency, meaning policy should care more about how fast an economy learns than how perfectly it allocates resources at a single point in time.
- From that perspective, the book defends industrial policy, infant-economy protection, and a more diffusion-friendly view of intellectual property as essential tools for building a high-learning society.

## Highlights

- The book is explicitly rooted in Kenneth Arrow’s 1962 "Learning by Doing" paper and extends that insight into a broad theory of growth and development policy.
- Knowledge is treated as a public good, which means markets alone will tend to underproduce and underdistribute learning.
- The authors argue for an "infant-economy" logic: countries should protect sectors where learning spillovers are strongest, especially manufacturing.
- A major claim is that restrictive intellectual-property systems can slow learning by enclosing the knowledge commons and limiting diffusion.
- The deeper policy message is that societies should optimize for learning capacity, not just for short-run allocative neatness or textbook free-trade purity.
