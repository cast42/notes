Title: Creating a Learning Society: A New Approach to Growth, Development, and Social Progress
Authors: Joseph E. Stiglitz and Bruce C. Greenwald

User-provided summary basis:
- The book argues that long-term growth depends less on capital accumulation than on a society’s ability to learn, innovate, and diffuse knowledge.
- It contrasts static efficiency with dynamic efficiency, arguing that policy should prioritize the rate of learning rather than merely the optimal allocation of existing resources.
- Key themes include knowledge as a public good, the infant-economy argument, industrial policy, criticism of restrictive intellectual property, and a more conditional view of free trade and globalization.

Publisher/book page details:
- Columbia University Press describes the book as taking Kenneth J. Arrow’s 1962 paper "Learning by Doing" as a starting point.
- It argues that market economies alone do not efficiently produce or transmit knowledge, and that government trade, industrial, financial, macroeconomic, and intellectual-property policies all shape learning.
- The book concludes with commentaries by Philippe Aghion, Robert Solow, and Kenneth Arrow.
