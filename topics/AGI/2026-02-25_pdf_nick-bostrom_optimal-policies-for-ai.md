# Optimal Policies for AI? (Nick Bostrom)

## TL;DR
Bostrom’s core claim (under a person-affecting framing): in many plausible parameter settings, it is better to reach superintelligence sooner rather than never, because the upside in life-years/quality is huge unless near-term extinction risk is extremely high. Operationally, he argues for **fast progress to capability**, followed by a **short, focused safety pause before broad deployment** (“swift to harbor, slow to berth”).

## Key takeaways
- A simple expected-value model can justify earlier AGI launch if immediate AI-catastrophe risk is below a high threshold.
- Adding discounting, risk aversion, and diminishing utility changes boundaries but not the central shape of results.
- Two-phase timing matters:
  - **Phase 1:** reach capability
  - **Phase 2:** safety/testing work once the real system exists
- Often-optimal strategy: brief post-capability pause (months to a few years), not indefinite delay.
- Long moratoria can backfire via underground races, militarization, overhangs, or accidental permanent relinquishment.
- Distributional lens: older/sicker populations and prioritarian ethics can favor earlier deployment due to immediate upside.

## Details
Bostrom compares baseline remaining life expectancy with potential post-AGI expectancy and examines when expected value favors launch now versus waiting. Across model variants, the robust pattern is that large long-run upside can dominate, provided near-term existential risk is not overwhelming.

The most practical section is the timing decomposition. Safety work is modeled as especially productive after capability exists (because you can test and refine against the actual system), which creates an argument for a short pause between “can build” and “widely deploy.” This generates a policy shape that is neither “ship instantly” nor “freeze forever,” but an adaptive, staged release posture.
