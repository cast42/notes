# The agent-shaped world

## TL;DR
The article argues that AI advantage comes from how your work is **structured**, not from hype or hustle. Work that is decomposable, measurable, documented, and exposed through clean interfaces is "agent-ready" and compounds faster. Teams that redesign workflows for agents will pull away before the gap becomes obvious.

## Key takeaways
- Agent leverage is mostly about **interfaces and process shape**.
- If tasks are explicit, instrumented, and modular, agents can execute and improve them.
- Feedback loops (measure → refine process → update prompts/tools) create recursive gains.
- Standardization is the unlock (containerization analogy): once interfaces are stable, automation scales.
- The current performance gap is often hidden because legacy workflows can still hit short-term metrics.
- Strategic question: what would it take to make your systems/processes “on agent,” like the shift to “online” before it?

## Details
The post frames an “agent-shaped world” as one where the biggest gains go to people and orgs that adapt their operating model for machine execution. The emphasis is not merely on using AI tools, but on redesigning underlying workflows so agents can reliably act on them.

A central analogy compares this to shipping containers: once a standard interface existed, transport and logistics could automate and scale across the system. Similarly, agent-native work depends on explicit contracts between data, tools, and tasks.

The warning is temporal: many incumbents still look healthy, so the shift feels slow — until compounding effects make the delta obvious.
