## TL;DR
Ramp’s March 2026 AI Index says overall business AI adoption reached a record **47.6%** in February, with **24.4%** of businesses now using Anthropic.

The headline shift is that Anthropic’s adoption surged while OpenAI’s business adoption rate declined, suggesting that enterprise preferences may be diverging from consumer-default assumptions.

## Key takeaways
- Overall business AI adoption: **47.6%**.
- Anthropic adoption: **24.4%** of businesses.
- Anthropic month-over-month growth: **+4.9%**.
- OpenAI adoption reportedly fell **1.5%**.
- Google grew slightly to **4.7%**; xAI remained under **2%**.
- Anthropic reportedly wins ~70% of head-to-head first-time business purchases vs OpenAI.

## Summary
Ramp presents March 2026 as a meaningful turning point in business AI adoption. OpenAI remains the most widely used model company overall, but the article’s main thesis is that Anthropic has moved from early-adopter favorite to mainstream enterprise contender at surprising speed.

The piece argues that this is not simply about raw model performance or price. Instead, it suggests Anthropic may have built a different kind of moat: cultural and organizational preference among technically influential users, which then spreads into enterprise buying behavior. That contrasts with OpenAI’s earlier advantage as the consumer default and Google’s bundle-based distribution via Workspace.

What makes the argument interesting is that Ramp treats enterprise adoption not as a pure benchmark or procurement story, but as something influenced by identity, trust, and cultural positioning. In that framing, Anthropic’s rise looks less like a standard feature battle and more like a shift in which company technically credible buyers want to align with.

## Why this matters
This is one of the stronger datapoints in your notes for enterprise AI adoption, especially for Anthropic vs OpenAI. It’s also useful context for broader questions about whether frontier-model competition is becoming as much cultural/distributional as technical.
