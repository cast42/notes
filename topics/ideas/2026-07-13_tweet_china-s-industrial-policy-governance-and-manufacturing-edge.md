# China’s industrial policy, governance, and manufacturing edge

*Patrick McGee — @PatrickMcGee_*

## TL;DR

- The quoted Financial Times passage argues that China’s manufacturing edge comes less from simple subsidies and more from a governance model that combines centralized strategic coordination with decentralized competitive pressure.
- Its core claim is that this state-led capitalist system has allowed China to build dense, vertically integrated supply chains and large-scale industrial clusters that are very hard for liberal democracies to reproduce quickly.
- A sharp supporting example is that most of BYD’s cost advantage over Tesla is attributed not to subsidies but to supply-chain control, industrial clustering, and economies of scale.

## Highlights

- The text argues that China’s manufacturing strength stems from the governance system that enables mass coordination and long-term industrial policy.
- A key nuance is that China mixes central control with decentralized competition among regions and firms for state backing.
- The passage contrasts this with liberal democracies, where accountability and decentralization make industrial coordination slower and harder.
- It explicitly says China’s edge is not mainly subsidy-driven: less than 15% of BYD’s per-vehicle cost advantage over Tesla is attributed to subsidies and favorable terms.
- The rest of the advantage is framed as structural: supply-chain control, industrial clustering, and economies of scale.
