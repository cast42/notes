I'm in Chongqing and it feels like walking through Cyberpunk 2077 as the main character. The city is built in layers, with streets running on top of streets. Chinese cities grow upward because they have to. In fact, China fits more than four times the population of the United States into almost the same amount of land. The skyscrapers are there for a reason.

I got here on a bullet train, and I can't stop noticing one sharp contrast. A few minutes outside the central station, the city turns into rice fields. Heavy industry and the job of feeding well over a billion people, sitting right next to each other. Food security comes up again and again here, and China is clearly good at planning around it. Beijing is playing a long game, and I don't think most people back home have noticed how fast this country is moving.

And I used to be one of them. I grew up thinking of China as cheap goods and copies. The idea was simple: the West innovates and China imitates. That idea is wrong, and it comes from not paying attention. It stopped being true a while ago.

You feel it the moment you're on the ground. One of the first things I noticed in China is how many young people are in STEM. Engineers everywhere. Everywhere I look, something is going up. (To be fair, they build at home too. It's just always the same project.)

And it's not just the number of people, it's what they've already built. Rural China has better infrastructure than the richest city in Italy. High-speed rail is everywhere, China's network is now longer than every other country's put together, and it reaches almost every city of any size. They built all of it in about seventeen years.

If rail is impressive, energy is the part Europe should really look at. Where we created energy insecurity, China did the opposite. They're building roughly half of all the nuclear reactors going up in the world right now, and they finish them in five to seven years, the last ones the US built took about fifteen. On top of that they're adding solar at a scale that's hard to picture. Italy, meanwhile, voted nuclear out of existence in two referendums (something our own government later called a "terrible mistake") and now pays some of the highest power prices in Europe. Cheap energy, a growing population, and the ability to actually build. It all feeds on itself. Fewer environmental rules let them move faster, and as much as I love nature, Europe will eventually have to loosen some of its own.

Power is one thing. Parts are another. If you build hardware, you already know Shenzhen. Whatever component you need, someone there can get it to you fast.

Seeing all this, I can't help comparing it to home. Europe has gone quiet. We learned to regulate instead of build. Most people who pursue higher studies go into law and finance, and most of us (me included, for a long time) have no real idea what it takes to make a physical thing. The result is stagnation and a strange lack of excitement about the future.

Part of it is that in Europe we're in love with the past. I moved to Naples at 14 and to Milan at 23, and they spent that whole time working on the same metro stop near my home. To be fair, in Italy every time you dig there's a good chance you hit something Roman or Greek, so we'd rather preserve everything than build anything new. It's beautiful. It's also a losing move in a world that changes at inference speed.

But I'm not pessimistic. Lately I've been talking to a lot of Italian builders, and they could be the spark. I recently met a guy tinkering at an Italian lab on autonomous research. That gives me hope and excitement. Things change the moment people realize how malleable the world is and start thinking in decades.

Europe is one half of the West. America is the other, and it's a weirder story. It's still the place to be, even if it sometimes feels like the whole country shrank down to two options: San Francisco, or one of Elon's companies. Say what you want about him, he's close to single handedly trying to rendustrialize a country that sent too much of itself overseas.

But the bigger American story is a quiet one of great dematerialization. Almost all the software I use is American. The US spent a generation of its best people on consumer apps, people who might have built hardware instead. Then it took the lead on intelligent machines, which is amazing. But I sometimes think that this lead won't hold unless America rebuilds the industrial base that made it strong in the first place.And that's really what I came here to think about: where China actually stands in AI. Beijing's goal is dead simple. Back in 2017, right after AlphaGo beat the world's best Go player, the government published the New Generation AI Development Plan: be the world's top AI power by 2030. That's it.

China can build, that part's obvious, but it's been behind on the hardest layer: chips. Semiconductors might be the most complicated supply chain humans have ever put together (think about everything that goes into a single ASML machine). In 2024, China spent more importing chips than it spent on oil. So the whole country is now focused on making that supply chain at home. A team in Shenzhen, reportedly led by former ASML engineers, has built a prototype of an EUV machine, the one tool that makes the most advanced chips, and which until now only ASML could build. It already produces the right kind of light. It can't make working chips yet, and realistic timelines point to 2028 or 2030. But "many years away" is starting to look optimistic.

Chips are only half the story. There's also data, and it matters just as much. Between WeChat and a country wired with sensors, China generates more of it than anyone. Kai Fu Lee made this point in AI Superpowers: this era rewards execution and data more than pure discovery, and that's exactly China's strength.

We got a glimpse of what all this adds up to when DeepSeek dropped R1 in early 2025, matching the best American models at a fraction of the cost. It was perhaps the West's Sputnik moment. I won't try to call who wins from here; I'm not qualified for that. But three things, watched from the ground, make me think China can pull even over time.

First, I don't think compute is the real ceiling. If the scaling laws hold, the constraint becomes energy, and you just saw who's building the most of it. At that point the AI race is mostly a giant engineering project, and only two groups have actually pulled off engineering at that scale: Elon, and the CCP.

Second, the skilled builders I trust are already doing impressive things with local Chinese models on their own machines.

Third,  Their latest DeepSeek model was the first to run on domestic chips like Huawei's Ascend and Cambricon on day one. It's not a fully homegrown stack yet but it's a signal.

None of this is a prediction. It's just what it looks like from the ground. The world is far more malleable than people think, and the ones who get that, and play the long game, tend to win. I came to China curious. I'm leaving convinced Europe needs to wake up, and quietly hopeful that we still can. We just have to remember how to build.

I'm going to go find some hotpot now.
