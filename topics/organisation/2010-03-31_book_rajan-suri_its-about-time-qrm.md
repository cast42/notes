# It’s About Time (Rajan Suri) — Quick Response Manufacturing

## TL;DR
Suri’s central thesis: **competitive advantage comes from time, not cost**. QRM (Quick Response Manufacturing) focuses on radically reducing lead time across the value chain; lower cost is mostly a downstream effect of faster flow.

## Core claim
Traditional management (and sometimes Lean implementations) over-optimizes local efficiency and cost. QRM instead optimizes **response time and flow** end-to-end.

## 5 key terms
1. Lead time
2. Flow
3. Focus
4. Capacity slack
5. Systems thinking

## Key concepts
### 1) MCT — Manufacturing Critical-path Time
Total elapsed time from order creation to delivery, including waiting/queue time.

### 2) The 80% capacity rule
QRM recommends deliberate slack (often ~20%).
At very high utilization, queueing delays rise nonlinearly; throughput looks “efficient” locally but becomes slow system-wide.

### 3) QRM cells
Cross-functional, end-to-end teams organized around flow (not departmental silos).

### 4) Time-based KPIs
Prioritize:
- lead time
- response time
- time-to-market
Over local utilization/efficiency metrics.

## QRM vs Lean (practical contrast)
- Lean often emphasizes cost/efficiency and variability reduction.
- QRM emphasizes time/responsiveness, deliberate slack, and absorbing variability.
- QRM is especially strong in high-mix, low-volume contexts.

## Radical implication
**Costs follow time.**
Shorter lead times reduce WIP, errors, rework, coordination burden, and ultimately total cost.

## Strategic lens
QRM combines:
- queueing theory
- systems thinking
- organization design
- behavioral dynamics under time pressure
