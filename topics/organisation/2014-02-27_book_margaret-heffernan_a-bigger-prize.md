# A Bigger Prize: Why Competition Isn't Everything and How We Do Better

## TL;DR

- Competition is a useful mechanism, but it becomes destructive when treated as the default answer to motivation, selection, innovation, and organizational design.
- Individual optimization can be collectively defeating: rankings and winner-take-all rewards hide costs imposed on colleagues, institutions, and future performance.
- Durable achievement depends on collaboration, trust, fairness, information sharing, and measures that operate across the whole system and over longer time horizons.

## Key takeaways

### Change the unit of performance

The book's Purdue-chickens case contrasts selection for individual output with selection for group output. It is a memorable example of a general management error: rewarding the strongest visible component can weaken the system around it.

### Competition has hidden system costs

Competitive scoring is attractive because it creates clarity, but the score can displace the real objective. Internal rivalry may increase visible individual effort while reducing help, trust, creativity, learning, and institutional resilience.

### Collaboration is an operating capability

Heffernan does not argue that all competition is bad. The stronger claim is that organizations underinvest in the structures that make cooperation productive: lower power distance, shared information, reciprocal help, safety, trust, and evaluation over longer periods.

### Success needs several dimensions and timescales

A single ranking at a single moment cannot capture whether a system is learning, distributing capability, preserving trust, or remaining healthy enough to perform tomorrow.

## Relevance to human–AI collaboration

The same error appears when an AI assistant is evaluated as an isolated performer. A strong model can produce impressive answers while the human–AI system performs poorly because goals, context, uncertainty, decision rights, or feedback are not shared effectively.

The better unit of analysis is the pair or team:

- Did the human and AI form a shared understanding of the goal?
- Did each contribute its complementary information and strengths?
- Were uncertainty and disagreement made inspectable?
- Did the collaboration improve the decision or completed work?
- Did it build trust and reusable knowledge for the next interaction?
