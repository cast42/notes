## TL;DR
Marc Hemeon highlights Jack’s argument that AI changes the economic logic of organizational hierarchy by collapsing the information-routing role historically played by middle management.

In this framing, companies can flatten toward three core roles: **ICs (builders)**, **DRIs (owners)**, and **player-coaches (builders who also lead)**.

## Hemeon’s post
“Middle management existed to pass information up and down the org chain.

AI removes this middle management layer and flattens orgs.

Great article suggests we only need 3 roles in an org:
ICs (builders), DRIs (owners), and player-coaches (builders who lead).”

## Summary of Jack’s article: *From Hierarchy to Intelligence*
Jack’s article starts from a historical observation: org charts are fundamentally **information-routing systems**. The Roman army, Prussian General Staff, American railroads, Taylorist management, wartime coordination, and postwar matrix organizations all solved the same problem—how to move information, decisions, and accountability across larger and larger groups of people despite human span-of-control limits.

The article traces that lineage in detail:
- **Romans** used nested units and defined commanders as an information protocol.
- **Prussia** invented a formal staff layer to support generals, effectively creating an early professional middle-management function.
- **Railroads** imported military hierarchy into business and formalized the modern org chart to prevent chaos and collisions.
- **Taylorism** optimized work inside the hierarchy through specialization and measurement.
- **The Manhattan Project** showed that cross-functional coordination could outperform rigid compartmentalization in high-stakes settings.
- **Matrix organizations** and later frameworks like McKinsey 7-S tried to balance central control with local agility.
- **Modern tech experiments** (Spotify squads, Holacracy, Valve) pushed against hierarchy, but struggled to scale cleanly.

The core argument is that these systems persisted because organizations lacked a better information-routing technology. AI changes that. If much of middle management’s role was to aggregate information, summarize context, precompute decisions, align units, and relay direction, then AI can increasingly perform those routing functions faster and at lower cost.

That does **not** mean leadership disappears. Instead, the article suggests orgs may simplify around a smaller set of roles:
- **ICs / builders** doing the work,
- **DRIs / owners** accountable for outcomes,
- **player-coaches** combining execution with coordination.

The deeper claim is that AI is not just a productivity tool inside the current corporation—it may be a new coordination substrate that changes the shape of the corporation itself.

## Why this matters
This is one of the clearest organizational-design arguments for AI as a structural force, not merely a task-level accelerator. If true, the impact is less “everyone works a bit faster” and more “the corporation needs fewer layers because routing and synthesis become software problems.”
