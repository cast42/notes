# The people do not yearn for automation

*Nilay Patel*

## TL;DR

- Nilay Patel argues that AI backlash is not mainly a marketing problem but a consequence of people experiencing AI as flattening, invasive, job-threatening, and misaligned with ordinary human life.
- His key concept is "software brain": a worldview that sees everything as databases, loops, and structured instructions, which works in software and enterprise workflows but breaks down when applied to people, law, government, and culture.
- Strong lens for AI adoption: the technology lands best where work is already software-shaped, while consumer resistance often reflects a rational refusal to make more of life legible, surveilled, and automatable.

## Highlights

- Patel’s central claim is that people do not yearn for automation, which helps explain the widening gap between tech-industry enthusiasm and public irritation with AI.
- AI adoption depends not only on model capability but on how much structure, data exposure, and behavioral standardization people are willing to accept.
- The essay argues computers should adapt to people, not require people to flatten themselves into databases so software can act on them.
