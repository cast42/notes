Title: The People Who Will Thrive in the AI Age
Author: The Atlantic

Summarize output:
The article argues that AI will not primarily divide people by raw intelligence, but by their relationship to mental effort. The key claim is that when intelligence becomes cheap and abundant, "volition is valuable": the winners will be people who use AI to stretch their minds, not just to avoid thinking. Drawing on workplace studies, the piece says AI has made work more intense rather than lighter, with more multitasking, more evening and weekend bursts, more messaging, and less focused uninterrupted time, a pattern some researchers call "AI brain fry."
*"When intelligence is plentiful, volition is valuable."*
The author groups people into three broad camps. "Productive Passengers" with low need for cognition use AI to make tasks easier, gaining productivity but risking cognitive atrophy; the article cites studies showing drops in brain connectivity, critical thinking, memory, and even task performance when AI is removed after short use. "Reluctant Optimizers" understand the risk but get pulled into convenience and efficiency anyway, gradually shifting from a cultivation mindset to an optimization mindset, where output matters more than excellence or self-formation. This group is especially vulnerable to "cognitive surrender," with one study finding humans accepted an AI's wrong answers 80% of the time.
The hopeful group is the "Mental Marathoners," people who enjoy hard thinking and will try to use AI as a tool for agency rather than as a replacement for judgment. The article recommends concrete habits: ask AI for hints rather than answers, start with a blank page, rotate AI and non-AI tasks, use tutors instead of generic chatbots when possible, and treat AI as a librarian rather than an oracle. The broader warning is that without institutional countermeasures, AI could create severe "cognitive polarization," splitting society into people who think more with AI and people who increasingly stop thinking for themselves. The proposed response is cultural and educational, not just technical: schools and organizations should focus less on sorting people by IQ and more on cultivating motivation, autonomy, competence, relatedness, and the desire to undertake difficult mental work.
*"The defining human features therefore are propulsions"*
