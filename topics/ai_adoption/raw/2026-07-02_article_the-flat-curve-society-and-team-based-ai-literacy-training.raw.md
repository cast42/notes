Summarize output:
Steve Yegge's argument is that AI capability is still rising fast behind the scenes, but for most people it will soon look flat. He thinks frontier models are approaching a level where governments and labs will tightly control access, so the public and most companies will be stuck with something like today's "Fable-class" systems while more powerful models stay gated. On top of that, humans hit two limits: a "demand horizon," where their problems are not hard enough to show differences between models, and a "discernment horizon," where model outputs become too hard to verify. In practice, that means the intelligence curve may still be exponential, but accessible, usable intelligence will feel like it has plateaued.
*"Superhuman means unverifiable."*
From there, the article shifts from prediction to organizational advice. If broadly available model capability stabilizes, then SaaS becomes more durable than people expected, because rewriting everything in-house with AI remains expensive, risky, and token-hungry. Yegge argues the real challenge for 2026-2027 is not waiting for better models but training people to work effectively with the ones they already have. He leans heavily on a Netflix training study that found three early cohorts of AI use, non-users, single-agent users at roughly 4M tokens per heavy-use day, and multi-agent users at roughly 12M to 15M, and says people could jump cohorts with about five hours of hands-on team training that used their real work. He highlights a striking retention result: 96% of trainees stayed in the second cohort for six weeks after the course.
The broader takeaway is that "AI literacy" is becoming a core business skill, and it has to be taught deliberately. First, companies need to get employees over the anxiety and resistance hump by teaching them how to spend tokens productively. Then they need a second layer of training around token efficiency, budgeting, routing work to the cheapest model that can handle it, and measuring outcomes instead of vanity usage. Yegge's view is that this plateau is not necessarily bad: it gives companies stable ground to build new engineering practices, supervised agentic workflows, and a more sustainable craft of using AI well rather than simply chasing each new model jump.
*"The plateau is not a bad thing. A plateau lets us set up a camp and start building."*
