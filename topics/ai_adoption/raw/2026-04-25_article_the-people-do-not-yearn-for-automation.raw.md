Article: THE PEOPLE DO NOT YEARN FOR AUTOMATION
Author: Nilay Patel
Site: The Verge / Decoder
URL: https://www.theverge.com/podcast/917029/software-brain-ai-backlash-databases-automation

Today on Decoder, I want to lay out an idea that’s been banging around my head for weeks now as we’ve been reporting on AI and having conversations here on this show. I’ve been calling it software brain, and it’s a particular way of seeing the world that fits everything into algorithms, databases and loops, software.

Software brain is powerful stuff. It’s a way of thinking that basically created our modern world. Marc Andreessen called it in 2011 when he wrote “Why software is eating the world.” But software thinking has been turbocharged by AI in a way that helps explain the enormous gap between how excited the tech industry is about the technology and how regular people are growing to dislike it more and more over time.

Patel points to polling showing strong AI backlash, especially among Gen Z, despite heavy usage of tools like ChatGPT and Copilot. His claim is that the industry misreads this as a marketing problem, when it is actually an experience problem. People already encounter AI constantly through search overviews, slop in feeds, workplace tools, and political fights over data centers.

The core concept is “software brain”: seeing the world as a set of databases that can be controlled with structured instructions. That mindset works well in businesses, software systems, and repetitive workflows. But it breaks down when applied to governments, law, communities, and ordinary human life. Databases are not reality, and people are not loops.

He uses several examples:
- DOGE taking control of government databases and running into the fact that the databases were not the government itself.
- The recurring fantasy that AI can replace lawyers, despite law being fundamentally ambiguous rather than deterministic.
- AI’s strong fit in enterprise settings, where much work already looks like repetitive data collection, analysis, and action loops.
- Smart home automation as a long-running example of something tech companies love more than regular people do.

A major part of the argument is that AI adoption asks people to make themselves legible to software. To get the most out of AI, they are asked to connect email, messages, calendars, files, workouts, and preferences across systems so models can build persistent memory and act on their behalf. Patel argues this is backwards: computers should adapt to people, not people to computers.

He argues that tech executives keep assuming better marketing will solve AI’s popularity problem, but you cannot market people out of their lived experience. If AI feels flattening, invasive, job-threatening, and energy-hungry, no amount of branding fixes that.

Useful frame for AI adoption:
- AI adoption is likely strongest where work is already software-shaped.
- Consumer AI resistance is not just ignorance or fear; it can be a rational rejection of being turned into structured data.
- The ceiling on AI enthusiasm may come less from model quality than from how much of human life people are willing to expose, standardize, and automate.

The essay’s key line is essentially that “the people do not yearn for automation.” That is the cultural counterweight to Silicon Valley’s belief that more software control is obviously progress.
