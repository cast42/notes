Article: We're in 1905: Why Electricity (Not Dot-Com) Is the Right AI Analogy
Author: Joe Reis
Source: Substack
URL: https://joereis.substack.com/p/were-in-1905-why-electricity-not

Core argument:
Joe Reis argues that the right analogy for AI adoption is not the dot-com bubble but electrification. Electric motors existed decades before productivity gains fully materialized, because factories initially swapped the power source while keeping the same layout and workflows. The real gains came only after factories were redesigned around the new technology.

AI parallel:
- Enterprises are often just bolting LLMs onto old processes: Copilot on SharePoint, chatbots on warehouses, agents around legacy flows.
- This creates the illusion of modernization without changing the underlying architecture or org design.
- Even AI-assisted code migration can be a trap if it simply recreates old systems faster.

Important concepts from the piece:
- The bottleneck is not the technology itself but organizational design, incentives, workforce skills, and institutional inertia.
- Centralized BI and warehouse-driven analytics are compared to old belt-and-pulley factory systems with a central shaft bottleneck.
- The AI-era equivalent of electrification’s “unit drive” is decision-making moving closer to where data is generated, with AI embedded into business processes rather than accessed through central request queues or portals.
- Roughly 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots failing to show measurable P&L impact is framed not as evidence that AI does not work, but that companies are still applying it inside the wrong architecture.

Key framing lines:
- “We’re in 1905.” The motor works, everyone has bought one, but almost nobody has redesigned the factory.
- Winners will not just have better tools. They will redesign the factory, or build a new one from scratch.
- Big incumbents may be outcompeted by smaller entrants that build AI-native operating models instead of layering AI onto legacy processes.

Interpretation notes:
- This is a useful frame for AI adoption because it shifts attention away from model novelty and toward workflow redesign, org change, and local decision-making.
- It also explains why adoption can feel both widespread and shallow at the same time: many firms have bought the motor, few have rebuilt the factory.
- The piece is strongest as an organizational and architectural metaphor, even if the exact timeline for AI transformation is debatable.
